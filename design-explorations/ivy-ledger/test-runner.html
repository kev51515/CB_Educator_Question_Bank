Degraded coral reefs are notably quieter than healthy ones: the snaps, grunts, and whistles produced by resident fish and invertebrates fade as those organisms disappear. Marine ecologist Aran Mooney and colleagues wondered whether this silence might itself impede recovery, since many reef larvae rely on sound to select a place to settle. To test the idea, the team installed underwater speakers on patches of degraded reef off the U.S. Virgin Islands and broadcast recordings made at a nearby thriving reef. Coral larvae settled on the acoustically “enriched” patches at rates up to seven times higher than on silent control patches. The researchers caution, however, that loudspeakers alone cannot rebuild a reef: settlement is only the first step, and the larvae must still survive on substrate that may remain inhospitable.
