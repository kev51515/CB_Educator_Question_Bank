For decades, marine biologists judged the health of coral reefs almost entirely by sight, counting fish and measuring living coral cover. Yet a healthy reef is also unmistakably loud: snapping shrimp crackle like static, damselfish grunt and whoop, and sea urchins scrape audibly as they graze.

Recording soundscapes at restored reefs in Indonesia, researchers found that this acoustic richness returned within a few years of coral transplantation, closely tracking the recovery seen in visual surveys. Crucially, drifting fish larvae appear to use these sounds to steer toward suitable habitat, suggesting that sound is not merely a symptom of reef health but a mechanism of its recovery. Some teams now broadcast recordings of thriving reefs at degraded sites—a technique called acoustic enrichment—to draw young settlers back.
