Healthy coral reefs are surprisingly loud places: snapping shrimp, grunting fish, and the crackle of feeding urchins combine into a soundscape that larval fish use to locate suitable habitat. Marine ecologist Aran Mooney and colleagues wondered whether degraded reefs, which fall comparatively silent, might be restored in part by replaying these missing sounds. Off the coast of the U.S. Virgin Islands, the team anchored underwater speakers to damaged reef patches and broadcast recordings made at thriving reefs nearby. Coral larvae settled on the sound-enriched patches at rates up to seven times higher than on silent control patches, suggesting that acoustic cues play a far larger role in reef recovery than previously assumed.

Adapted from a 2024 study on reef bioacoustics published in Royal Society Open Science.
